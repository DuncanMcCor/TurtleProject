1. Species & Conservation Status

The Alabama Red-bellied Turtle, or **Alabama Red-bellied Cooter** (*Pseudemys alabamensis*), is an emblem of Alabama's unique biodiversity.

Conservation Status

  • **Official State Reptile of Alabama.**
  • **U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service Status:** Endangered (since 1987).
  • **IUCN Red List:** Endangered (EN).
  • It has one of the smallest distributions of any North American turtle.

Key distinguishing features include its highly arched shell (carapace), the striking **pale yellow to reddish/orange plastron** (under shell), and a prominent notch at the tip of the upper jaw, bordered by tooth-like cusps.

2. Habitat & Distribution

Geographic Range:

The turtle's range is extremely restricted, centered primarily in the **Mobile-Tensaw River Delta** in:

  • Mobile County, Alabama
  • Baldwin County, Alabama
  • Adjacent to Mobile Bay.

They may also be found in coastal areas of extreme southeastern Mississippi.

Image Placeholder: The critical Mobile-Tensaw Delta ecosystem.

Preferred Habitat:

They prefer shallow, slow-moving aquatic environments that are rich in vegetation, specifically:

  • Fresh to **brackish** (slightly salty) water.
  • Shallow backwaters of rivers, bayous, and bays.
  • Areas with soft, sandy bottoms.
  • Extensive beds of aquatic plants (like hydrilla and eel-grass), which they use for cover, food (they are primarily herbivorous), and basking substrate.

3. Life History: Nesting Habits

Female Red-bellied Turtles leave the water to find nesting sites, which is often the most dangerous activity for them due to road mortality.

Season

Nesting occurs from **April to early August**, with the peak activity usually in July.

Location

Nests are dug on **dry land** in open, sparsely vegetated areas with **sandy soil** (e.g., river banks, levees, or dredge spoil sites like Gravine Island).

Clutch Size & Hatching

Females lay about **4 to 9 eggs** per clutch. Hatchlings emerge in the fall (Sept-Nov) or may **overwinter** in the nest and emerge in the following spring.

Threats at Nesting Sites:

Nest predation is high, with major threats coming from raccoons, fish crows, wild boars, and the invasive red imported fire ant.

4. Movement & Tracking Clues

How They Move:

  • **Aquatic Movement:** They are excellent swimmers (natatorial) and move easily through the slow-moving delta waters, often foraging in dense vegetation.
  • **Basking:** They spend significant time basking on exposed logs or snags to regulate their body temperature (thermoregulation). They are very wary and will submerge quickly if disturbed.
  • **Terrestrial Movement (Nesting):** The critical movement observed on land is the female's journey to and from the nesting site (sandy river banks/levees). This is where tracks are most likely to be found.

Footprints for Tracking:

Since specific *Pseudemys alabamensis* footprint photos are rare, here are the tracking clues to look for in the sandy nesting areas:

  • **Tracks:** A meandering, asymmetrical trail marked by the movement of the four feet. The rear feet often leave a wider drag mark than the front feet.
  • **Belly Drag:** On soft, loose sand (like a dune or river bank), a wide, continuous, central drag mark will be visible from the bottom of the shell (plastron) scraping the ground.
  • **Claw Marks:** Look for distinct, short, parallel scrape marks (3-5 toes) on either side of the central drag mark.
  • **Nesting Site Clues:** Females leave a disturbed, circular area where they dug the shallow nest chamber, often covered up carefully to conceal the eggs.

6. Conservation Spotlight

The biggest man-made threat to the Alabama Red-bellied Turtle is **road mortality**. Females are often killed while crossing highways (like US 98 / Battleship Parkway) to reach nesting sites on higher ground.

A Conservation Success: Road Fencing

In the Mobile-Tensaw Delta, a chain-link fence was installed along critical sections of the causeway to separate the delta from the bay. This has proven highly effective, significantly **reducing the deaths of nesting females and hatchlings** on the highway.

Other vital efforts include:

  • **Habitat Protection:** Safeguarding the shallow, vegetated backwaters they rely on for food.
  • **Predator Management:** Protecting known nesting sites from high-volume egg predators like raccoons and crows.
  • **Monitoring:** Continuing to track population trends to ensure the species is recovering.
