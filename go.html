Alabama's Endangered Turtles: The Gopher Tortoise

A high school research project on $\textit{Gopherus polyphemus}$

1. Keystone Species & Protection Status

The Gopher Tortoise is one of North America's oldest reptile species and the only tortoise native to the Southeastern U.S.

Status in Alabama

  • Federally **Threatened** in the western counties (Mobile, Washington, Choctaw).
  • State Protected: A species of **High Conservation Concern** statewide.

It is considered a **Keystone Species** because its burrows provide refuge for over 360 other species, including snakes, frogs, and insects.

Source: Outdoor Alabama Facts

2. Habitat & Range

The Longleaf Pine Ecosystem

Gopher Tortoises live in the **Coastal Plain** region of Alabama. They prefer dry, upland habitats with **deep, well-drained, sandy soils**â€”perfect for digging their elaborate burrows.

Their habitat is historically the **Longleaf Pine-Wiregrass Sandhills**. This environment requires periodic natural fire to keep the forest canopy open, allowing sunlight to reach the ground for the growth of low-lying herbaceous food plants.

Range in Alabama

They are found in counties including Mobile, Baldwin, Clarke, Coffee, Covington, Escambia, Geneva, Henry, Houston, and many others in the southern half of the state.

3. Movement and Tracking

Digging and Shelter

The Gopher Tortoise's life revolves around its burrow. Using its powerful, **shovel-like forefeet**, it digs tunnels that average 15 feet long and 6.5 feet deep, though some can exceed 40 feet.

Movement outside the burrow is mainly for foraging for food (grasses, leaves, fruit) or basking in the sun. They are slow and steady, using their sturdy, **elephantine hind feet** to walk on land. They are **not** equipped for aquatic life.

The Burrow Apron

Look for the **burrow apron**, a distinctive mound of loose, fan-shaped sand at the entrance. The entrance itself is shaped like a **half-moon** and is usually about the same width as the tortoise's shell.

Tracking (Footprints)

  • **Front Prints:** Shovel-like, wide, and distinctive.
  • **Rear Prints:** Stumpy, more rounded, like small elephant feet.
  • **The Trail:** Look for a staggered, walking pattern with parallel lines. In very loose sand, you might see a faint, continuous line where the bottom of the shell (plastron) or tail drags.

4. Nesting Habits

Reproduction and Hatching

  • **Mating** occurs from April to June.
  • **Nesting** typically takes place in May or June. The female usually digs a nest cavity right in the **burrow apron** where the sand is loose and sunny, ensuring the eggs get enough heat.
  • She lays only one **clutch** (group of eggs) per year, containing 5 to 15 eggs.
  • **Incubation** lasts about 80 to 100 days.

Temperature and Sex

Like many reptiles, the sex of the Gopher Tortoise hatchlings is determined by the incubation temperature. This is called **Temperature-Dependent Sex Determination (TSD)**. Warmer nests produce females; cooler nests produce males.

Hatchlings are tiny and vulnerable, often seeking shelter in the parent's burrow before digging their own small tunnel.

Gopher Tortoises and their burrows are federally and state protected. It is illegal to disturb, harm, or collect them. Observe them from a respectful distance.
