Loggerhead Sea Turtle: Caretta caretta

Endangered Species Research for Alabama High Schools

1. Species Information & Status

The Loggerhead is named for its exceptionally large head and massive jaw muscles, which allow it to crush the hard shells of prey like conchs and crabs. They are the most common sea turtle species found nesting in the United States.

  • Scientific Name: Caretta caretta
  • Average Adult Size: Approx. 92 cm (36 inches) in shell length.
  • Average Adult Weight: 113 kg (250 lbs).
  • Appearance: Reddish-brown, slightly heart-shaped carapace (top shell) and a pale yellowish plastron (bottom shell).
Conservation Status:
  • U.S. ESA: Threatened (specifically the Northwest Atlantic Ocean population segment found in Alabama).
  • IUCN Red List: Vulnerable worldwide.

2. Habitat & Alabama Distribution

Loggerheads are widely distributed across the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. They favor coastal areas, bays, lagoons, and the continental shelf, where they forage for food.

Focus on Alabama:

Loggerheads are the most common sea turtle to nest on Alabama's beaches. Their critical habitat areas include:

  • Nesting Beaches: Gulf Coast beaches in Mobile and Baldwin counties, including areas near Fort Morgan, Gulf Shores, and Bon Secour National Wildlife Refuge.
  • Marine Habitat: Shallow coastal waters, estuaries, and river mouths along the Alabama Gulf Coast.

3. The Journey Home: Nesting

Female Loggerheads exhibit remarkable "natal homing," often returning thousands of miles to nest on the same general beach where they were born.

Nesting Cycle Details:

  • Nesting Season (AL): May 1st through August.
  • Hatching Season (AL): July through October.
  • Nesting Frequency: Females nest approximately 4 times per season.
  • Clutch Size: Typically lay 80–120 soft-shelled eggs per nest.
  • Incubation: Eggs incubate for about 55–60 days.

Locating the Ocean:

Hatchlings instinctively crawl toward the brightest horizon, which, on a natural beach, is the moonlight reflecting off the ocean. Artificial light from coastal development is a major threat, as it disorients hatchlings, causing them to crawl inland where they perish.

4. Movement & Locomotion

Aquatic (Swimming)

Loggerheads are powerful swimmers, using their forelimbs in a strong, flapping motion adapted for long-distance migrations between feeding areas and nesting grounds. They often ride ocean currents, covering thousands of miles.

  • Spend about 85% of their time underwater.
  • Typical dive duration is 2-45 minutes.
  • Navigate using the Earth's magnetic field.

Terrestrial (Crawling)

When females crawl onto the beach to nest, they use an **alternating** pattern, moving one front flipper followed by the opposite rear flipper, then repeating the process. This is the key difference when identifying their tracks.

5. Tracking & Resources (Footprints)

Loggerhead Track Identification:

The tracks (crawls) left by a Loggerhead female on the beach are highly distinctive and used by conservation teams for nest monitoring:

  • Flipper Marks: Marks are comma-shaped and **alternating**. This pattern leaves a distinctive, wavy line down the center of the track.
  • Track Width: The track is relatively wide, averaging **70 to 90 cm (28 to 35 inches)**.
  • Tail Drag: Crucially, a Loggerhead track usually has **NO** thin, straight, well-defined tail drag mark down the center (unlike the Green turtle).
  • "False Crawl": Look for signs of sand thrown backward; an abandoned attempt is called a "false crawl."
